Elementary Algebra

A firm understanding of the elementary rules that govern arithmetic will serve you well. Each rule stacks on top of others, it's better to see what's happening rather than memorizing.

Fractions & Rational Arithmetic

A fraction \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) (with \(b \neq 0\)) is simply \(a\) multiplied by the multiplicative inverse of \(b\), written \(b^{-1}\). Every rule for fractions follows from this definition together with the standard field axioms.

Equivalent Fractions

Multiplying numerator and denominator by the same nonzero value \(c\) does not change the fraction.

\[\frac{a}{b} = \frac{ac}{bc}, \quad c \neq 0\]

Why?

\(\dfrac{ac}{bc} = ac \cdot (bc)^{-1} = ac \cdot c^{-1} \cdot b^{-1} = a \cdot (cc^{-1}) \cdot b^{-1} = a \cdot 1 \cdot b^{-1} = \dfrac{a}{b}\)

Multiplication of Fractions

\[\frac{a}{b} \cdot \frac{c}{d} = \frac{ac}{bd}\]

Why?

\(\dfrac{a}{b} \cdot \dfrac{c}{d} = (a \cdot b^{-1})(c \cdot d^{-1}) = (ac)(b^{-1}d^{-1}) = (ac)(bd)^{-1} = \dfrac{ac}{bd}\)

Addition of Fractions

\[\frac{a}{b} + \frac{c}{d} = \frac{ad + bc}{bd}\]

Why?

We need a common denominator to combine the terms. Using the equivalent fractions rule, rewrite both fractions with denominator \(bd\): \[\frac{a}{b} = \frac{ad}{bd}, \qquad \frac{c}{d} = \frac{bc}{bd}\] Now the denominators match and we can add numerators: \[\frac{ad}{bd} + \frac{bc}{bd} = \frac{ad + bc}{bd}\]

Division of Fractions

\[\frac{a}{b} \div \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a}{b} \cdot \frac{d}{c} = \frac{ad}{bc}\]

Why?

Dividing by a number is identical to multiplying by its reciprocal. So \(\frac{c}{d}\) becomes \(\frac{d}{c}\), and we get: \[\frac{a}{b} \div \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a}{b} \cdot \frac{d}{c} = \frac{ad}{bc}\]

Complex Fractions

A fraction whose numerator or denominator is itself a fraction. The strategy is to multiply through by the LCD of all inner fractions.

\[\dfrac{\,\dfrac{a}{b}\,}{\dfrac{c}{d}} = \frac{a}{b} \cdot \frac{d}{c} = \frac{ad}{bc}\]

Exponent Rules

The exponent \(a^n\) is defined for positive integer \(n\) as repeated multiplication: \[a^n = \underbrace{a \cdot a \cdot a \cdots a}_{n \text{ times}}\] Every exponent rule below is a direct consequence of counting factors.

Product Rule

\[a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}\]

Why?

\(a^m \cdot a^n\) is \(m\) copies of \(a\) multiplied by \(n\) copies of \(a\), giving \(m + n\) copies total: \[a^m \cdot a^n = \underbrace{a \cdots a}_{m} \cdot \underbrace{a \cdots a}_{n} = \underbrace{a \cdots a}_{m+n} = a^{m+n} \quad \]

Quotient Rule

\[\frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}, \quad a \neq 0\]

Why?

Expanding and cancelling \(n\) common factors of \(a\): \[\frac{a^m}{a^n} = \frac{\overbrace{a \cdots a}^{m}}{\underbrace{a \cdots a}_{n}} = \underbrace{a \cdots a}_{m-n} = a^{m-n} \quad \]

Power Rule

\[(a^m)^n = a^{mn}\]

Why?

\((a^m)^n\) is \(n\) copies of \(a^m\) multiplied together. By the product rule applied repeatedly: \[(a^m)^n = \underbrace{a^m \cdot a^m \cdots a^m}_{n} = a^{m+m+\cdots+m} = a^{mn} \quad \]

Zero Exponent

\[a^0 = 1, \quad a \neq 0\]

Why?

Apply the quotient rule with \(m = n\): \[\frac{a^n}{a^n} = a^{n-n} = a^0\] But any nonzero quantity divided by itself equals \(1\), so \(a^0 = 1\).

Negative Exponent

\[a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}, \quad a \neq 0\]

Why?

From the product rule, \(a^n \cdot a^{-n} = a^{n + (-n)} = a^0 = 1\). So \(a^{-n}\) is by definition the multiplicative inverse of \(a^n\), which is \(\dfrac{1}{a^n}\).

Fractional Exponent

\[a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}\] \[a^{m/n} = \sqrt[n]{a^m} = \left(\sqrt[n]{a}\right)^m\]

Why?

We need \(a^{1/n}\) to be consistent with the power rule. If we raise it to the power \(n\): \[\left(a^{1/n}\right)^n = a^{(1/n) \cdot n} = a^1 = a\] So \(a^{1/n}\) is a number whose \(n\)-th power is \(a\) which is exactly the definition of the \(n\)-th root \(\sqrt[n]{a}\).

For \(a^{m/n}\), apply the product of exponents: \(a^{m/n} = (a^{1/n})^m = (\sqrt[n]{a})^m\).

Power of a Product and Quotient

\[(ab)^n = a^n b^n\] \[\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)^n = \frac{a^n}{b^n}\]

Why?

Expanding \((ab)^n\) as repeated multiplication and regrouping: \[(ab)^n = \underbrace{(ab)(ab)\cdots(ab)}_{n} = \underbrace{a \cdots a}_{n}\cdot\underbrace{b \cdots b}_{n} = a^n b^n \quad \]

Radical Rules

Radicals are fractional exponents in disguise: \(\sqrt[n]{a} = a^{1/n}\). Every radical rule is therefore a restatement of an exponent rule. This section states them explicitly in radical notation, since that form is common in practice. Cross-referencing with the exponent rules shows exactly why each holds.

Product Rule for Radicals

\[\sqrt[n]{ab} = \sqrt[n]{a} \cdot \sqrt[n]{b}, \quad a, b \geq 0\]

Why?

Rewrite using fractional exponents and apply the power-of-a-product rule: \[\sqrt[n]{ab} = (ab)^{1/n} = a^{1/n} b^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}\cdot\sqrt[n]{b} \quad \]

Quotient Rule for Radicals

\[\sqrt[n]{\frac{a}{b}} = \frac{\sqrt[n]{a}}{\sqrt[n]{b}}, \quad a \geq 0,\ b > 0\]

Why?

\(\sqrt[n]{\dfrac{a}{b}} = \left(\dfrac{a}{b}\right)^{1/n} = \dfrac{a^{1/n}}{b^{1/n}} = \dfrac{\sqrt[n]{a}}{\sqrt[n]{b}}\))

Raising a Radical to a Power

\[\left(\sqrt[n]{a}\right)^m = \sqrt[n]{a^m} = a^{m/n}\]

Why?

\(\left(\sqrt[n]{a}\right)^m = \left(a^{1/n}\right)^m = a^{m/n} = (a^m)^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a^m}\))

Rationalising the Denominator

A denominator containing a radical is considered unsimplified. We eliminate it by multiplying numerator and denominator by a suitable expression (using the equivalent fractions rule, so the value is unchanged).

\[\frac{a}{\sqrt{b}} = \frac{a}{\sqrt{b}} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{b}}{\sqrt{b}} = \frac{a\sqrt{b}}{b}\]

Logarithm Rules

The logarithm is the third face of the same relationship that gives us exponents and radicals:

\[a^b = c \iff \log_a c = b \iff a = c^{1/b}\]

In words: \(\log_a c\) asks "to what power must we raise \(a\) to get \(c\)?" Every logarithm rule is a restatement of an exponent rule through this definition. The base \(a\) must satisfy \(a > 0\) and \(a \neq 1\).

Product Rule

\[\log_a(xy) = \log_a x + \log_a y\]

Why?

Let \(p = \log_a x\) and \(q = \log_a y\), so \(x = a^p\) and \(y = a^q\). Then: \[xy = a^p \cdot a^q = a^{p+q}\] Taking \(\log_a\) of both sides: \(\log_a(xy) = p + q = \log_a x + \log_a y\)

Quotient Rule

\[\log_a\!\left(\frac{x}{y}\right) = \log_a x - \log_a y\]

Why?

With \(x = a^p\) and \(y = a^q\) as before: \[\frac{x}{y} = \frac{a^p}{a^q} = a^{p-q}\] So \(\log_a\!\left(\dfrac{x}{y}\right) = p - q = \log_a x - \log_a y\)

Power Rule

\[\log_a(x^n) = n\log_a x\]

Why?

Let \(p = \log_a x\), so \(x = a^p\). Then: \[x^n = (a^p)^n = a^{pn}\] So \(\log_a(x^n) = pn = n \log_a x\)

Special Values

\[\log_a 1 = 0\] \[\log_a a = 1\] \[a^{\log_a x} = x\] \[\log_a(a^n) = n\]

Why?

All follow directly from the definition. \(a^0 = 1 \implies \log_a 1 = 0\). \(a^1 = a \implies \log_a a = 1\). The last two express that \(\log_a\) and the exponential base \(a\) are inverse functions.

Change of Base Formula

\[\log_a x = \frac{\log_b x}{\log_b a}\]

Why?

Let \(y = \log_a x\), so \(a^y = x\). Take \(\log_b\) of both sides: \[\log_b(a^y) = \log_b x \implies y \log_b a = \log_b x \implies y = \frac{\log_b x}{\log_b a}\] Substituting \(y = \log_a x\) gives the formula

This is useful because calculators typically only provide \(\log_{10}\) and \(\ln\). For example: \[\log_2 7 = \frac{\ln 7}{\ln 2} \approx \frac{1.9459}{0.6931} \approx 2.807\]

Sidenote on Logarithms

Before John Napier invented logarithms, multiplying large numbers of the kind astronomers and navigators dealt with daily was agonisingly slow and error-prone. Logarithms changed everything. Because log(a×b) = log(a) + log(b), they turned multiplication into addition. You could look up two values in a table, add them, and look the result back up. A calculation that took hours now took minutes. Entire books of log tables were printed and distributed. Ships navigated more accurately. Astronomers mapped the sky faster. It's no exaggeration to say logarithms accelerated the Scientific Revolution all from a single algebraic property.
